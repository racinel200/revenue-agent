Airbnb / Short-Term Rental ROI: the math, explained

Before you trust any calculator — including ours — with a purchase decision, it helps to know what it's actually computing. Here are the four numbers that matter most, and how to get them by hand.

Net Operating Income (NOI)

Everything starts here. NOI is your rental income minus operating expenses — but before your mortgage payment, because financing is a purchase decision, not a property performance metric.

NOI = Annual Rental Income − Operating Expenses
(operating expenses: cleaning, management fee, utilities, insurance, property tax, maintenance reserve, platform fees — NOT the mortgage)

Cap Rate

Cap rate tells you the property's return as if you'd bought it in cash — useful for comparing deals to each other regardless of how each buyer plans to finance.

Cap Rate = NOI ÷ Purchase Price

A short-term rental's cap rate is usually higher than a long-term rental's on the same property, because nightly rates outrun monthly rent — but so do the operating costs (cleaning, management, utilities), so don't compare an STR cap rate to a long-term-rental cap rate without adjusting for that.

Cash-on-Cash Return

Cap rate ignores financing. Cash-on-cash return doesn't — it tells you the return on the actual cash you put in.

Annual Cash Flow = NOI − Annual Mortgage Payments
Cash-on-Cash Return = Annual Cash Flow ÷ Total Cash Invested
(total cash invested: down payment + closing costs + furnishing/setup costs)

Break-Even Occupancy

The occupancy rate at which your cash flow hits exactly $0 — below it, you're paying to own the property; above it, it's paying you. Solving for it directly (rather than eyeballing a few scenarios) requires isolating occupancy in the cash-flow equation:

Break-Even Occupancy = Fixed Costs ÷ (ADR × (1 − variable cost %) × 30.4 days)
(fixed costs: mortgage, insurance, fixed utilities, property tax ÷ 12 — anything that doesn't scale with bookings. Variable cost %: cleaning + platform fees as a share of each night's revenue.)

Run this before you make an offer, not after — if a listing's marketed occupancy assumption is only a few points above your break-even, you have very little margin for a slow season.
